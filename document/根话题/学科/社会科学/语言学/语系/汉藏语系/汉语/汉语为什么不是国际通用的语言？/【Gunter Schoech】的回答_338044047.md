 <h2>As a foreigner who writes in English on Zhihu, I have my ideas</h2>
 <p data-pid="2DoaTCdr">非常抱歉我不会用中文作答或写作。如果可以的话我也希望自己能用中文交流。我现在正在努力学习中文，但是还完全不能书写，也说不了多少。我的母语是德语，并且能够非常熟练地使用英语和法语，能够比较熟练地使用西班牙语（包括口语和书写），并且我学了6年的拉丁语，还会说一点波兰语。我在尝试减少语言障碍。</p>
 <p data-pid="xVqXct58"><a href="https://www.zhihu.com/people/janny-16-13/activities%EF%BC%89%E4%B8%8B%E9%9D%A2%E7%9C%8B%E5%88%B0%E4%B8%80%E9%83%A8%E5%88%86%E7%BF%BB%E8%AF%91" class="internal">https://www.zhihu.com/people/janny-16-13/activities）下面看到一部分翻译</a></p>
 <p class="ztext-empty-paragraph"><br></p>
 <p data-pid="ML4ydvly">Having said this, let me give you my theory:</p>
 <ol>
  <li data-pid="-zetOiBD">In a sense, Chinese IS an international language. That is due to the big Chinese population, not only in China itself, but all over the globe.<br>
   People of Chinese origin are to be found all over South East Asia, but also in a host of other countries. <br>
   The "China Towns" are a visual sign. <br>
   Even in my German university, the RWTH Aachen, the Chinese students were the biggest minority already in 1993 when I started my studies there. <br><br>
   This is of course possible because ~1 out of 5 people living on this planet is Chinese, the Chinese had strong trade relations in South East Asia, and even smaller waves of emigrations always make up a large number of people in absolute terms.</li>
  <li data-pid="pNl57ZVd">However, as far as I can tell, the people speaking Chinese in such an international context, are mainly also ethnic Chinese. What is much more rare is to find the locals learning Chinese to interact with such Chinese. <br><br>
   In order for Chinese to become a true international language, it would have to be chosen as <b>"lingua franca", which means a language that is adopted as a common language between speakers whose native languages are different.</b></li>
 </ol>
 <p data-pid="C_UkSB1R">Why this has not happened is at the heart of this question. <br>
  I have mainly 2 arguments:</p>
 <p data-pid="2R1yqFHt">First Historic, Second suitability</p>
 <ol>
  <li data-pid="0WKJJNmj">I am no historian or linguist, but think that the main historical difference of Chinese and other likely contenders is the relative lack in colonial expansion. <br>
   This is actually something very nice to say about the Chinese people: While there have been epic power struggles over thousands of years within the Chinese territory, conflicts with outside powers were mainly defensive. Just take the Great Wall as a symbol: It is a completely defensive instrument. Wars that China did fight with foreign powers were typically with nomadic tribes from central Asia, who were the aggressors.<br>
   As a consequence, the language remained mainly confined to the Chines territory. <br><br>
   Sure, there were the notable exploits of Zheng He for example. But they did not lead to colonization. On the contrary, the next emperor made all explorations illegal, as ships with more than 1 mast. <br>
   So China went back to it's core belief: Zhong Guo: <br>
   If you are the "Middle Kingdom", literally "the center of the earth", and far far larger and mightier than anybody around you, how much sense does it make to venture further? China's attitude has over centuries and millennia been: "Let them come to us",<br>
   maybe pay tribute and then do some trade, or maybe not even that. China has this tradition of closing itself more or less to the rest of the world, and depending when you look in history, it could very well afford that, while at others, this meant it fell behind. <br><br>
   European nations were often very different: The Portuguese as first big explorers were a tiny, very poor and marginalized country, the opposite of the "Zhong Guo", as they did not have access to the Mediterran. So they had to seek far and wide. Spain is a little bit different case: Having been occupied by Moorish invaders for 700 years or so, they simply didn't stop when they finally managed to kick them out: By coincidence, Christoper Columbus discovery happened in the very same year as the last Moorish fortress (Granada) fell in Spain. <br>
   England was very much like Portugal, poor and marginalized, in the North Sea. <br>
   Attracted by the new gained wealth of the Spanish and Portuguese, the "followed the money trail". Similar the Dutch, who in the 17th century were the leading seafaring nation. <br>
   The French were a little later, and focused on Africa, but that's still the common language there, and gains French international status. <br><br><br>
   All this ship-building, exploring, conquering and colony building lead to vast empires. <br><br>
   British Empire<br><br>
   At its height it was the <b>largest empire</b> in history and, for over a century, was the foremost global power. Still by 1922 the <b>British Empire</b> ruled over about 458 million people, one-quarter of the world's population at the time, and covered more than 33,700,000 km2, almost a quarter of the Earth's total land area.<br><br>
   This of course covers the decisive time of the industrial revolution and a first wave of globalization. We might see tighter interactions today, but the directions were set there and then. <br></li>
 </ol>

 <p data-pid="R0lvZ8nt">What's not on the map, but also key: The USA as ex-colony have dominated world history in the 20th century. And they happen to have adopted English. When I took an American history class during my high school year in the US, I learned that when the young US had to decide for a language, there was actually a majority for German. I think ~40% of US immigrants are of German origin. But through a bribe, English won the vote. Who knows, if the world might be a different place today. I remind you, I learned this in the US, not in Germany.</p>
 <p data-pid="eKJs0Hys">We Germans were even later, only uniting in 1871, and losing all colonies in WW I. <br>
  This left the scientific world, where in the 1920's, German was the lingua franca, given that so much was published in German. However, Nazi prosecution of the Jews, including Jewish Germans, put an end to that. An Albert Einstein in the USA will write in English, not German anymore.</p>

 <p data-pid="xb9A1zi2">So there are alternatives for global languages.</p>
 <p class="ztext-empty-paragraph"><br></p>
 <p data-pid="PFky2bsq">2. The second reason I can give is "suitability": <br><br>
  If foreigners want to adopt a language as lingua franca, how hard is that to do?<br><br>
  Here, I speak from personal experience. My mother tongue is German, a "Germanic" language. <br>
  English is usually also considered "Germanic" language, although it is a big mix. <br><br>
  That was my first "living" foreign language, as I had studied the "dead" language Latin before.</p>
 <p data-pid="LJTVC2P-">I then learned French, and later Spanish, so 2 "Romanic" or "Romance" languages. <br>
  I also learned some Polish, a "Slavic" language, and very different from all the others, closely related to Russian.</p>
 <p data-pid="ZCTiZKjY">Last but not least, I am trying to learn Chinese.</p>
 <p data-pid="km-dNEJJ">I have already written on Zhihu about my experiences with these languages.</p>
 <p data-pid="-KgCM687">E.g. here <a href="https://www.zhihu.com/question/45951228/answer/283497181" class="internal">Gunter Schoech：如何用英语学德语？</a></p>
 <p data-pid="COstsmV_"><a href="https://www.zhihu.com/question/21768003/answer/310904718" class="internal">为什么法国人英语普遍较差，而德国人英语大多不错？</a></p>
 <p data-pid="ITr4f9VY">In short, in terms of grammar, worst is Polish, then close second German. <br>
  Chinese has very very little grammar by comparison (before you angrily reply me, please read my article above: Many people only understood what grammar actually is, when they read this. Because Chinese has so little of it).</p>
 <p data-pid="SyrseVRg">-&gt; So then, Chinese should be easy? Well, no. For me, it is, with Polish, the most difficult to learn. The reasons are</p>
 <p data-pid="ayErULyZ">a) the writing. As it is not based on an alphabet, the effort to learn it is much greater. <br>
  Chinese children spend a lot of time to learn all the characters, and I guess you never fully stop. <br>
  My 6 year old son reads German and French without much difficulty, and his 4 year old brother has already picked up a little from him.</p>
 <p data-pid="yPEeRh8z">b) the relative scarcity of different "sounds". There are only a little over 100 sounds in Chinese. Multiply that by 4 (or 5 incl. silent) tones, and you get &lt;&lt; 1000 combinations. But already for the simplest of newspapers you need &gt; 2000. So your end up with orally identical sound which have various meanings. For disambiguation, you have either the written language (with the problem in a), or the context, preferably by adding another word with many meanings, but only in this one , wanted, meaning, they overlap.</p>
 <p data-pid="Gv7fpS9n">For your native speakers, this comes naturally. But I find that to learn Chinese, you have to live it and breathe it every day, on site. Then you can make those links fast enough between words. But you can't really learn that in a language class far a way, or worse, in home study. <br>
  In fact, I taught myself Spanish, from 0 to a level which allowed me to fly to the capital, with my suitcase in hand, and start living there: find a place to live, to go university etc. <br>
  I don't think that would ever be possible with Chinese for me.</p>
 <p data-pid="5Rz8l_GQ">When people study Chinese in Germany, they say: I study "Sinology". That includes speaking and writing, maybe a little History on the side. But essentially, they study Chinese. Nothing else.</p>
 <p data-pid="1OaLtjMt">Imagine, your entire studies consisted of CET 4 or let it be CET 6. But nothing else. You will say: "I study electrical engineering", and CET 4 is just something that comes with it.</p>
 <p data-pid="5NIda6wA">It seems, as lingua franca, Chinese is just too difficult.</p>
 <p data-pid="RAoPu8Ls">And if you take most of the populations that did not grow up with English, they are still much closer to learning English than learning Chinese. <br>
  Portuguese is very close to Spanish, so you can add Brazil. With that, you have the American Continents 100% complete.</p>
 <p data-pid="hjczFEeT">Even Hindi is an Indo-Germanic language (common roots)</p>
 <p data-pid="MXeT0B_z">So I assume that the growing role of China will not reverse what the historic developments set up before.</p>

 <p data-pid="dtF-RihJ"><a href="https://www.zhihu.com/question/268309261/answer/338848560" class="internal">中文为什么不是国际通用的语言？</a></p>
 <p data-pid="mUJ8v6Fd"><a href="https://www.zhihu.com/people/hunanhunan" class="internal">满洲第一巴图鲁</a></p>
 <p data-pid="MBDBgqmz">混子。</p>
 <p data-pid="0dlxvLLs"><a href="https://www.zhihu.com/people/gunter-schoech" class="internal">Gunter Schoech</a> 冈特答主的回答还是挺有意思，闲来无事，我给大家翻译一下，不足的地方大家指正。</p>
 <p data-pid="7rr0yUZU">说到这个，让我来谈谈我的理论：</p>
 <p data-pid="AtsL5-H9">1. 某种意义上，中文其实是一门国际通用语。这主要是因为中国庞大的人口，不仅限于中国国内，也包括世界各地的华人。</p>
 <p data-pid="5ifYJEj_">华人不仅遍布东南亚，在许多其他国家也都可以看到华人的身影。</p>
 <p data-pid="SOTT8rEz">“唐人街”就是一个很直观的标志。</p>
 <p data-pid="0Kb_aVMR">我1993年开始在德国的亚琛工业大学读书。就算是在那个时候，中国学生就已经成为学校里最大的少数族裔群体了。</p>
 <p data-pid="GbAcD7VS">当然，这个与中国庞大的人口基数脱不开关系，毕竟，中国人口占世界人口的1/5。中国人在东南亚有着牢固的贸易关系。绝对意义上讲，就算是很小的移民潮也会涉及到大量的人口。</p>
 <p class="ztext-empty-paragraph"><br></p>
 <p data-pid="OXD7RZa8">2. 但是，据我所知，在这样的国际环境下，说中文的人群仍旧以华裔为主。你很难发现会有当地人专门去学习中文和这些华裔交流。</p>
 <p data-pid="He75sTt2">如果想让中文成为一门真正的国际通用语的话<b>，它首先要被选做“通用语”（lingua franca），意思就是说让讲不同母语的人选它做为他们之间的共同语。</b></p>
 <p data-pid="NmOSRQ5J">中文为何没有被选做通用语才是这个问题的核心。</p>
 <p data-pid="AgmJqvyp">对此，我主要有两个论点，第一个是历史上的，第二个是适合性方面的。</p>
 <p data-pid="1FTuN4N-">（1） 我并非历史学家或者语言学家，但是我觉得中国人与其他的可能的竞争者在历史上最主要的不同在于中国较少有殖民扩张。</p>
 <p data-pid="TVT-WInk">虽然几千年来中国内部不乏严酷的权利斗争，但是对外方面，中国的姿态大多都是防御性质的。比如说长城：长城完全就是一个防御性设施。中国对外的主要敌人一般都是来自中亚的游牧民族，他们本身就是侵略者。</p>
 <p data-pid="eD8E2mzg">因此，中文的使用主要被局限在中国境内。</p>
 <p data-pid="3kAquDmq">中国确实也做过一些值得一提的探险，譬如说郑和下西洋。然则，他们的目的不是殖民。相反，下一任君主实施锁国政策，一桅以上的船只一律不得出海。所以，中国又重新回到它的核心理念“中国”上来了。</p>
 <p data-pid="3id8bivC">“中国”意为世界的中央。如果你已经是世界的中央了，比周围所有的国家都要辽阔而强大，那么再去冒险开拓能有多大意义呢？几千年来，中国对外的态度就是“远人来之”，其他国家兴许来朝贡然后做点儿贸易，哪怕这个都没有也无妨。中国或多或少有着闭关锁国传统。纵观历史，中国也确实能够很好地自给自足，然而比较而言，这意味着中国已经落后了。</p>
 <p data-pid="YGR1B0m8">欧洲国家的做法往往是与中国完全不同的：葡萄牙人是第一批大规模的开拓者。葡萄牙是一个弱小、贫穷且边缘化的一个国家，由于它无法触及到地中海，所以它的环境可以说与“中国”这个概念是八竿子打不着。正因如此，它们需要四面八方地去寻找、开拓。西班牙的情况又有一些不同：西班牙被摩尔人殖民者占据了700年之久，它们在最终赶走殖民者之后并未止步：碰巧，在摩尔人最后的要塞格拉纳达攻克的那一年，哥伦布发现了新大陆。</p>
 <p data-pid="LPj4XMsa">英格兰的情况很像葡萄牙，位于北海之上，贫穷且边缘化。</p>
 <p data-pid="coHy3---">西班牙人和葡萄牙人获得的大量财富吸引了英国人，他们紧接着踏上了“淘金之路”。荷兰人与英国人类似，他们在17世纪成为了头号海洋强国。 法国的行动更晚一点儿，他们把重点放在了非洲。时至今日，法语仍旧是非洲的通用语言，法语也获得了国际通用语言的地位。</p>
 <p data-pid="3xl7moVR">造船、探险、侵略以及建立殖民地，靠着这些，巨大的帝国建立起来了。</p>
 <p data-pid="z38Nw5iS"><b>大英帝国</b></p>
 <p data-pid="f8-wdedu">大英帝国是世界历史上<b>最大的帝国</b>，它曾占据世界霸主地位长达一个多世纪之久。到1922年，大英帝国治下的人口有4.58亿之多，占到当时世界人口的四分之一。它的领土面积达33，700，000平方公里之多，几乎占到全球四分之一的陆地总面积。</p>
 <p data-pid="50mPOkl8">这其中穿插这具有决定性作用的工业革命时代和第一次全球化浪潮。我们现在或许能看到世界在更加紧密的交流，但是大方向在当时就已经定下。</p>
 <p class="ztext-empty-paragraph"><br></p>
 <p data-pid="rYOBL0s0">美国的崛起虽然不能从地图上看出来，但是仍旧十分重要：美国作为一个前殖民地地区，在20世纪的时候成为全球霸主。而偏偏美国也采用了英语。在我中学时期上美国历史课的时候，我学到当时美国刚刚建国需要选择官方语言的时候，美国其实有不少的德裔人口。我觉得至少40%的美国移民来自德国。可是英国的家伙们居然在投票上行贿，赢得了游戏，输掉了人品。谁知道嘞，要不然今天真指不定是个美丽新世界呢。提醒一下，这个可不是我在德国学到的，是我在美国听说的。</p>
 <p data-pid="q2ZCBrME">我们德国人的崛起就更晚了，1871年才完成统一，在一战中失去所有的殖民地。</p>
 <p data-pid="si_Sxyjf">这时就只剩下科学领域了，十八世纪二十年代，德语成为科学界的通用语，大部分学术著作都是以德语出版的。好景不长，纳粹对犹太人的驱逐（其中也包括说德语的犹太人）为这一切画上句号。爱因斯坦们在美国用英语写作，再没德语什么事儿了。</p>
 <p class="ztext-empty-paragraph"><br></p>
 <p data-pid="_OZndgcN">所以这些就是有可能成为全球通用语的语言。</p>
 <p class="ztext-empty-paragraph"><br></p>
 <p data-pid="wFW4Uh7Z">（2） 第二个原因，我觉得是“适合性”： 如果外国人要选择一门语言作为通用语的话，这有多难？</p>
 <p data-pid="odWNfS_W">我来说说我的个人经历吧。我的母语是德语，属于日耳曼语族。 英语虽然是个“大杂烩”，但是通常也被认为属于日耳曼语族。</p>
 <p data-pid="YxEO2oBG">英语是我的第一门“活”外语，因为我之前还学了拉丁语，拉丁语是一门“死”语言。</p>
 <p data-pid="jgNKzi4r">然后我开始学习法语，之后是西班牙语，这两门都属于罗曼语族。</p>
 <p data-pid="Qpln5KTk">我还学了一些波兰语，那是一门“斯拉夫”语言，它与其他几门语言都完全不同，与俄语很像。</p>
 <p data-pid="DI6VlpMt">最后，当然也同样重要，我在试着学习中文。</p>
 <p data-pid="SUXlMDlD">我已经在知乎上用这些语言来分享我的经历啦~</p>
 <p data-pid="SWdfcQUs">你看这儿！</p>
 <p data-pid="DrqVRBZR"><a href="https://www.zhihu.com/question/45951228/answer/283497181" class="internal">Gunter Schoech：如何用英语学德语？</a></p>
 <p data-pid="w6g8e2CP"><a href="https://www.zhihu.com/question/21768003/answer/310904718" class="internal">为什么法国人英语普遍较差，而德国人英语大多不错？</a></p>
 <p data-pid="05YbR4V2">总而言之，语法方面，波兰语最难，紧接着是德语。比较来看，中文几乎没什么语法（先别怼我，可以先看看我之前写的文章：很多人看了这些之后才正儿八经明白语法到底是个什么东西。因为汉语中确实没什么语法）。</p>
 <p data-pid="gJX9l5-P">所以，中文就会很容易是不？错！怼我来说，中文和波兰语是最难学的两门语言！原因如下：</p>
 <p data-pid="7_KSnR81">一，书写。由于中文不是基于字母表的，所以我学习起来得下很大功夫。</p>
 <p data-pid="KogAWKF-">中国的小朋友会花很多时间学习汉字，我猜你们从没完全停止过学习汉字。</p>
 <p data-pid="NCzYu_z1">我儿子才六岁，小家伙阅读德语和法语已经没什么问题了，他四岁的弟弟还能跟他学上一点儿。</p>
 <p data-pid="NVZg4PTk">二，中文中的“音”比较少。中文中只有差不多100个音。配合4个音调（说5个也行，如果算上轻声的话），中文可以组合出差不多1000多个音。哪怕是要读懂最简单的中文报纸，也至少需要2000多个词。所以，中文同样的读音可能有许多不同的意思。为了消除歧义，无论是在书写中（像第一条中提出的问题一样），还是在语境当中，中国人喜欢把两个都具有许多意思的字叠加到一起，而只有在这个新的组合词当中，这两个字的意思是相同的。</p>
 <p data-pid="PT2WToHm">对于母语使用者来说，这是自然而然的。但是我发现当我在学习汉语的时候，我得时时刻刻都要生活在这种语言环境之中才可以学着快速在词与词之间建立联系。但是，如果你在一个很远的语言班里学习的话，缺少这种环境，就很不妙了，如果在家自学的话，就更加困难了。</p>
 <p data-pid="tsUcD7Ik">事实上，我曾自学西班牙语，从零基础一直自学到我可以飞到西班牙首都，提着行李箱，开始在那里居住：找到一个住的地方，上大学等等。</p>
 <p class="ztext-empty-paragraph"><br></p>
 <p data-pid="Olviu81v">如果是中文的话，我觉得我可能不能做到。</p>
 <p data-pid="saBxKHGv">当人们在德国学习中文的时候，他们会说：我在学习“汉学”。其中包括说和写，可能还得夹杂着一些历史。但是，本质上来说，他们只是在学习汉语，没有什么别的东西。</p>
 <p data-pid="6wC0skdb">想象一下，你的学习其实只包含大学四级或者大学六级，除此之外别无其他。你却说：“我学的是电气工程”，四级仅仅是他的一个附带品而已”.</p>
 <p data-pid="Dl2na7BO">这样来看，中文要成为一门通用语的话，确实有点儿难。</p>
 <p data-pid="NYBIBijP">哪怕我们只拿那些非英语国家的人们来说事儿，学习英语仍旧比学习中文要更加亲民一点儿。葡萄牙语和西班牙语很像，加上巴西，它有整个美洲大陆。</p>
 <p data-pid="0n7CWSJH">就算是海地语，也是一种印度日耳曼语系语言 （同源）。</p>
 <p data-pid="DH3EEjHj">所以，我猜测，虽然中国在世界上的地位与日俱增，但是可能也很难在语言方面打破这种既定的历史发展。</p>
